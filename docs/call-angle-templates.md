# Call Angle Templates: Discovery Call Scripts for Security Hiring Leads

## Overview

This document provides 4 reusable call angle templates tailored to the 4 different "Why Hiring Signal" categories in your Airtable base.

Each template includes:
- **Personalized opening** (30-45 seconds) that acknowledges their specific signal
- **Discovery goal** (what you want to learn)
- **Key discovery questions** (3-5 open-ended questions with reasoning)
- **5-step call arc** (natural flow from research → discovery → pain point → suggestion → commitment)
- **Common objections** (5-7 typical responses + your reply)
- **Customization tips** (how to personalize beyond template)
- **Post-call workflow** (what to update in Airtable)

---

## Template 1: Growth Signal

### Best For
Companies hiring due to **expansion, funding, new markets, new products, or headcount growth.**

**Indicators:** Series B funding, new office openings, "We're expanding our team," acquisition announcements, new product launches.

---

### Opening (30-45 seconds)

> "Hi [Name], it's [Your Name] from [Your Company]. I saw [Company Name] closed your Series B / announced your [new market/product] — congrats, that's exciting. I imagine that kind of growth brings a lot of new demands on your security infrastructure and team. I'm not trying to sell anything today, but I thought it was worth 20 minutes to understand how you're thinking about security as you scale, and what the mandate is for the CISO/security leader you're bringing on. Sound fair?"

### Discovery Goal

**What you want to learn:**
- How fast is the company growing? (Revenue, users, geographic expansion)
- How is the security team scaling alongside business growth? (Building new, hiring experienced?)
- What's the mandate for the new hire? (Catch up on basics? Build modern program? Scale existing?)
- What's the biggest gap right now? (Process, tooling, team, strategy?)
- Timeline: When do they need the new hire in place and demonstrating impact?

**Why it matters:** Growth companies are adding complexity (new systems, new geographies, new data) without always having security architecture to match. There's often a gap between business speed and security maturity. That gap is your value prop.

---

### Discovery Questions (3-5 key questions)

#### Question 1: Growth Context
**Q:** "Walk me through the growth you're seeing—what's driving it most right now? New markets, new products, or just scaling existing business?"

**Why ask:** Sets context for what's new/risky. If they're entering new markets (Singapore, Europe), that's new compliance risk. If new products, new infrastructure risk.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- Rapid growth = security playbook may be outdated
- Geographic expansion = regulatory/compliance new requirements
- New product = new data types or infrastructure

---

#### Question 2: Team & Mandate
**Q:** "In terms of security, what's the mandate for the person you're hiring? Are they building from scratch, or inheriting a team and modernizing?"

**Why ask:** Reveals what resources they have and urgency level. "Building from scratch" (very new to security) vs. "modernizing" (established but needs upgrade) changes your approach.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Building from scratch" = Foundational needs (policies, architecture, compliance basics)
- "Inheriting small team" = Team scaling + mentorship
- "Modernizing mature program" = Transformation, tooling, operational excellence

---

#### Question 3: Biggest Gap
**Q:** "If I asked you what the biggest gap is right now—in your security posture or infrastructure—what would you say? Is it people, process, tools, or something else?"

**Why ask:** This is where pain lives. Pain = urgency = willingness to engage. Most fast-growing companies have multiple gaps, but one always hurts most.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "People gap" = Need to hire, build team
- "Process gap" = Compliance, incident response, architecture
- "Tooling gap" = SIEM, SOAR, identity, data protection
- "Strategy gap" = No roadmap, unclear priorities

---

#### Question 4: Timeline & Approval
**Q:** "What's your timeline? When does the new security lead need to be up to speed and driving impact?"

**Why ask:** Reveals urgency and approval cycle. "Immediate" = hot lead. "Next quarter" = lower urgency.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- Pressing deadline = external (compliance, audit, customer demand) or internal (funding round, expansion deadline)?
- Who needs to approve investments = CISO (if exists), CFO, CEO?

---

#### Question 5: Current State
**Q:** "Where are you today on the basics—do you have a security framework in place, or are you more starting from foundational policies and architecture?"

**Why ask:** Clarifies maturity level and scope of work ahead. Foundation-building is different conversation than scaling.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Starting basics" = Policies, architecture, training
- "Got basics, need to scale" = Tools, automation, team
- "Mature but scaling" = Transformation, centers of excellence

---

### 5-Step Call Arc

#### Step 1: Research & Acknowledge (1-2 min)
**You:** Open with personalized reference to their growth.
- "I saw you announced Series B / opened Singapore office / launched new product"
- "That kind of growth is exciting and also puts pressure on your infrastructure team"
- "I imagine you're thinking about how to keep security pace"

**Prospect:** Nods, confirms the growth context.

---

#### Step 2: Discovery (8-10 min)
**You:** Ask questions 1-3 above.
- Listen 70%, talk 30%
- Take notes on their answers
- Ask follow-ups based on what they say

**Prospect:** Explains growth trajectory, team situation, biggest gap.

**Key listening:** Where's the pain? What keeps them up at night?

---

#### Step 3: Probe the Pain (5-7 min)
**You:** Once they mention a gap, probe deeper.
- If "process gap": "What does that look like today? How is it impacting you?"
- If "people gap": "How many people are you trying to hire? What's taking so long?"
- If "tooling gap": "What tools do you have now, and what's missing?"

**Prospect:** Gets more specific. Realizes they do have a real problem.

---

#### Step 4: Suggest (if appropriate, 3-5 min)
**You:** Light suggestion of how you might help.
- DO NOT pitch hard or claim to solve everything
- Focus on how OTHER companies like them approached the gap
- Example: "Most companies at your growth stage struggle with [gap]. Some start with [approach], others go for [approach]. Depends on your constraints."

**Prospect:** Sees you've helped others in similar situations.

**Important:** This step is SOFT. You're building credibility, not selling.

---

#### Step 5: Close & Next Step (2 min)
**You:** "This has been really helpful. I think there's a real opportunity here to dive deeper—not necessarily to sell you anything, but to understand your priorities better and see if we should talk more. Would it make sense to grab 30 minutes next week to map out a roadmap?"

**Prospect:** Says yes or gives condition.

**If yes:** "Perfect. Let me send a calendar invite. In the meantime, is there any prep you'd like me to do? Any specific area you want to go deep on?"

**If "maybe":** "Totally fair. How about I send you something [specific: article, case study, benchmarking data] and we reconnect next week?"

---

### Common Objections & Responses

#### Objection 1: "We're hiring internally; we don't need outside help."
**What it means:** They're building their own team, don't see external need yet.

**Your response:**
> "Got it—you're bringing on someone to lead the build. That's great. Even as you build, you'll want to avoid the common pitfalls other fast-growing companies hit. Have you seen any benchmarking on how long it typically takes to build out [their gap area]? Most companies underestimate. Would be worth a quick call to compare timeline expectations."

**Why it works:** Acknowledge their path, but introduce the idea that external validation/benchmarking has value.

---

#### Objection 2: "We're all set with our security tools/partner already."
**What it means:** They think you're selling security software.

**Your response:**
> "No, I'm not trying to replace what you have—sounds like you've got tooling sorted. What I help with is the program side: architecture, team structure, roadmaps, how to sequence the build as you scale. The tooling is part of it, but usually after you know what you're building for. Is the architecture and sequencing already planned, or is that still TBD as you bring on leadership?"

**Why it works:** Reframe from "tool" to "guidance/structure." Appeals to someone building something.

---

#### Objection 3: "We'll revisit this after we close the [funding round / product launch / acquisition]."
**What it means:** Timing is off; they're in crisis mode right now.

**Your response:**
> "Makes sense—you've got a lot on the plate. Here's the thing though: the time to plan security is BEFORE you scale further. Most companies regret waiting until after the milestone to think about it. How about we grab 20 minutes in 3 weeks, after [milestone]? That way you're planning ahead instead of catching up. Fair?"

**Why it works:** Agree with their timeline, but add urgency. Plant the seed that early planning saves headaches.

---

#### Objection 4: "We're not ready to hire for this yet."
**What it means:** Either timeline is wrong OR they don't feel pressure yet.

**Your response:**
> "Totally possible. But I'm curious—in your head, when do you think you'll be ready? And what would need to change between now and then for that to make sense?"

**Why ask:** Find out if it's truly "not ready" (move to 90-day follow-up) or if they're underestimating urgency (prove urgency).

---

#### Objection 5: "Can you send me something and follow up later?"
**What it means:** They're not immediately interested, but open to future conversation.

**Your response:**
> "Absolutely. Let me send over [specific asset, e.g., 'growth company security maturity benchmark' or 'series B security playbook']. What's the best email? And when makes sense to follow up—two weeks? A month?"

**Why it works:** Deliver something valuable, set specific follow-up date (don't just vanish).

---

#### Objection 6: "Who are you and what company are you from?"
**What it means:** You didn't establish credibility in opening.

**Your response:**
> "Good question. I'm [Name] from [Company]. We work with fast-growing security teams—companies going through growth like you are—on scaling their security programs without slowing down the business. I noticed [specific detail about their growth], and thought it might be worth a conversation. Have you got 15 minutes?"

**Why it works:** Brief introduction, establish credibility (you work with similar companies), remind of relevance (their specific growth).

---

#### Objection 7: "Not the right time; call me in 6 months."
**What it means:** They're deprioritizing now.

**Your response:**
> "Got it. Quick question though—what has to happen between now and then? Like, what needs to change for this to become a priority in 6 months?"

**Listen:** If they say "We'll have hired the CISO by then," fine—6-month follow-up makes sense. If they say "Budget," push back: "Gotcha. Typically budget planning starts 3-4 months before spend. So we should probably reconnect in 2-3 months to get in the budget conversation. Sound better?"

### Customization Tips

#### Tip 1: Reference Their Specific Growth
Don't say "I saw you're growing." Say:
- "I saw you closed your $25M Series B" (know the amount)
- "I noticed you announced operations in Singapore" (know the expansion)
- "I saw you launched [specific product]" (know what's new)

**Why:** Shows research + shows you're not just mass-calling.

---

#### Tip 2: Name the Person Who Hired Them (if possible)
If you found that a new CEO, CTO, or CISO joined recently:
- "I noticed [Name] joined as your CTO last month—that's a big signal about direction."
- "I saw [Name] left his role at [Previous Company]. Sounds like you're bringing on serious expertise."

**Why:** Shows deep research, builds rapport.

---

#### Tip 3: Reference Company-Specific Risk or Compliance
If you know their industry:
- FinTech: "You're handling customer financial data—PDPA and SOX considerations are different for you."
- Healthcare: "HIPAA and patient data adds a layer others don't deal with."
- E-commerce: "Customer data scale and payment processing—compliance is intense."

**Why:** Proves you understand their world, not a generic pitch.

---

#### Tip 4: Tailor Questions to Their Growth Type
- **If geographic expansion:** Ask about compliance in new jurisdictions
- **If new product:** Ask about new data types and infrastructure
- **If acquisition:** Ask about integrating security postures
- **If funding:** Ask about investor expectations on security

**Why:** Shows you've thought about THEIR specific type of growth.

---

#### Tip 5: Use Their Website/Blog Language
If their website mentions "scaling responsibly" or "trust-first approach":
- "I noticed on your site you talk about scaling responsibly. Part of that is making sure security keeps pace, right?"

**Why:** Reflects their values back at them. Feels like you understand their culture.

- ✅ They confirmed specific growth underway (funding, expansion, etc.)
- ✅ They articulated 1-2 real gaps (team, process, strategy)
- ✅ They committed to a follow-up meeting or next conversation

## Template 2: Compliance Signal

### Best For
Companies hiring due to **regulatory mandate, compliance deadline, or certification goal** (PDPA, ISO 27001, SOC 2, SOX, etc.).

**Indicators:** "ISO 27001 certification goal," "Compliance audit coming up," "New regulation deadline," "Data privacy officer hire," "Compliance program build-out."

---

### Opening (30-45 seconds)

> "Hi [Name], it's [Your Name] from [Your Company]. I noticed [Company Name] is ramping up on compliance—I saw the ISO 27001 commitment / PDPA preparation / audit coming up. That's exactly the kind of work I help companies with: building compliance programs under time pressure. I'm not pitching anything today, but I thought 20 minutes to understand your compliance timeline and what the new hire's mandate is could be useful. Sound fair?"

### Discovery Goal

**What you want to learn:**
- Which specific regulation or standard? (PDPA, ISO, SOC 2, SOX?) And what's the deadline?
- Is this proactive (company choosing) or reactive (mandate/audit forcing it)?
- How mature is their compliance program today? (None, partial, need to modernize?)
- What's the plan to get there? (Hire people, buy tools, process change?)
- Who's the decision-maker and what's their authority level? (Audit committee? CFO? CISO?)

**Why it matters:** Compliance is deadline-driven. Companies with hard deadlines (Q3 2026 audit, Q4 2026 certification) are motivated. There's budget. There's urgency. Compliance is also complex—most companies underestimate effort, timeline, and cost.

---

### Discovery Questions (3-5 key questions)

#### Question 1: Compliance Requirement
**Q:** "Help me understand the compliance mandate you're facing. Is this PDPA, ISO 27001, SOC 2, something else? And what's driving it—customer requirement, regulatory deadline, or internal decision?"

**Why ask:** Clarifies what they're implementing and urgency. PDPA = Singapore-specific, legal mandate. ISO = third-party audit. SOC 2 = customer requirement. Different playbooks.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- PDPA (Singapore) = Legal deadline, affects all orgs handling data
- ISO 27001 = Third-party certified audit; takes 6-12 months
- SOC 2 = Customer-driven; enterprise customers often require
- SOX = Financial services / public companies; heavy

---

#### Question 2: Timeline & Current State
**Q:** "What's your deadline for this? And where are you starting from—do you have a compliance program in place, or is this basically from scratch?"

**Why ask:** Reveals urgency and scope. "ISO cert by Q3 2026" is tight. "From scratch" is bigger scope than "refresh existing."

**How to listen:** Look for:
- Less than 6 months to deadline = High urgency, will need quick decisions
- 6-12 months = More time but still motivated
- No specific deadline = Lower urgency; might be "nice to have"
- "From scratch" = Need to build policies, controls, processes, evidence
- "Already have some" = Need to mature, expand, audit

---

#### Question 3: Team & Capability Gap
**Q:** "In terms of team, where's the gap? Do you need to hire people, bring in external help, upskill existing team, or all of the above?"

**Why ask:** Reveals whether their problem is people, process, or tools. Each needs different solution.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Need to hire" = Building compliance function (get budget, timeline for hire)
- "Need external help" = Open to bringing in consultants (your value prop)
- "Upskill existing" = Have people but lacking expertise (training/framework need)
- Usually it's all three.

---

#### Question 4: Decision-Making & Approval
**Q:** "Who's driving this on your side? Is it the CFO, CISO, Audit Committee, or someone else? And do they have budget approval already?"

**Why ask:** Finds real decision-maker and confirms budget exists. Compliance initiatives usually have budget; question is who controls it.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "CFO" = Budget holder, focused on cost/risk
- "CISO" = Security-led, focused on actual security posture
- "Audit Committee" = Governance-led, formal process
- "Budget approved" = Hot lead. No budget = slower.

---

#### Question 5: Success Definition
**Q:** "When this is done, what does success look like to you? Is it passing audit, certification in hand, or something else?"

**Why ask:** Clarifies what winning looks like and constraints. "Audit pass" is different from "ISO cert" (cert requires ongoing compliance).

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Pass audit" = One-time event (can be less rigorous, but risky)
- "Get certification" = Ongoing commitment (more rigorous, more value)
- "Use as customer requirement" = Performance-based
- "Demonstrate board compliance" = Governance-focused

---

### 5-Step Call Arc

#### Step 1: Research & Acknowledge (1-2 min)
**You:** Open with personalized reference to their compliance need.
- "I saw you're aiming for ISO 27001 certification by Q3"
- "I noticed the PDPA compliance mandate and your timeline to prepare"
- "I saw audit coming up in Q4; I imagine you're ramping up prep now"

**Prospect:** Nods, confirms compliance pressure.

---

#### Step 2: Discovery (8-10 min)
**You:** Ask questions 1-3 above.
- Listen 70%, talk 30%
- Take notes on deadline, scope, team gap
- Ask follow-ups on what's blocking them

**Prospect:** Explains compliance requirement, timeline, team situation.

**Key listening:** How tight is the deadline? Is it real (audit scheduled) or aspirational (goal)?

---

#### Step 3: Probe the Constraint (5-7 min)
**You:** Once they mention a blocker, probe deeper.
- If "timeline too tight": "How much time do you have realistically? What's eating time?"
- If "team too small": "How many compliance people do you need? How long to hire?"
- If "don't know what to do first": "Have you scoped the work, or is that still TBD?"

**Prospect:** Gets more specific. Realizes scope is bigger than they thought.

---

#### Step 4: Suggest (if appropriate, 3-5 min)
**You:** Light suggestion of how others tackled it.
- "Most companies at your size underestimate timeline. Typically [standard] takes [typical time frame]."
- "The common path is: first you map controls, then you evidence them, then you do readiness assessment, then audit. Sequence matters."
- "Some companies try to do this entirely internally; others bring in advisors for the first 2-3 months to set them up right, then hand off to internal team."

**Prospect:** Sees you've helped others.

**Important:** Position yourself as guide, not savior.

---

#### Step 5: Close & Next Step (2 min)
**You:** "I think there's real value in understanding your specific situation better—timeline, scope, team capacity. Should we schedule 30 minutes to walk through a realistic roadmap?"

**Prospect:** Says yes or gives condition.

**If yes:** "Perfect. I'll send calendar invite. Before we talk, can you share [specific document, e.g., 'audit scope' or 'compliance policy inventory']? Helps me prep."

**If "maybe":** "Fair. Can I send over [asset: PDPA roadmap template, ISO 27001 timeline guide, SOC 2 scoping document]? That might help you scope this out. Can I follow up next week?"

### Common Objections & Responses

#### Objection 1: "We're hiring a compliance officer to handle this."
**What it means:** They think it's a hire problem, not a guidance problem.

**Your response:**
> "That's smart—you definitely need someone leading this. Here's what I've seen: new compliance officers often get 3-4 months in before they realize the scope is bigger than expected, the timeline is tight, and they need external support to actually hit the deadline. How about we meet after you bring them on—say in 4-6 weeks—and we make sure you're set up for success from day one?"

**Why it works:** Acknowledge their plan, but suggest early alignment prevents delays later.

---

#### Objection 2: "This is all internal; we don't need outside help."
**What it means:** They think they can do it alone.

**Your response:**
> "Could be. Depends on your timeline and current capability. Quick question: have you scoped the full work yet? Like, do you know exactly what needs to happen between now and [deadline] and who owns each piece?"

**If they say "yes":** "Great. Then you probably know if you've got the internal capacity. If you ever need a gut check or external validation on roadmap, happy to help. Can I share [specific resource]?"

**If they say "no":** "That's the thing—most companies underestimate scope until they actually start. That's where external eyes are really useful: scope it right the first time, then you execute internally."

---

#### Objection 3: "Budget is tight right now."
**What it means:** They're cost-conscious or budget hasn't been allocated.

**Your response:**
> "Understood. Here's the reality though: compliance is cheaper to do right upfront than to redo after you fail audit. Most companies find that investing in the first month on scoping and roadmap saves them money and headaches later. What does budget approval look like on your end? Who decides?"

**Why it works:** Reframe cost as investment with ROI (avoid failed audit). Find budget holder.

---

#### Objection 4: "We'll revisit this after [current project] wraps up."
**What it means:** They're deprioritizing now.

**Your response:**
> "I get it; you've got stuff on the plate. But here's the thing: if your deadline is [Q3], you probably want to start scoping this now, even if you're not executing full-tilt yet. How about we grab 20 minutes in 2 weeks, before things get even more hectic? That way you at least have a realistic roadmap."

**Why it works:** Emphasize that planning now saves chaos later.

---

#### Objection 5: "We're already working with [consulting firm] on this."
**What it means:** They've already engaged a partner.

**Your response:**
> "That's great—you're serious about this. Quick question: are they helping with roadmap and strategy, or more execution/auditing? And are they helping with the compliance culture piece, or just the controls?"

**If they say "full-service":** "Perfect. In that case, no need for another voice. If that ever changes or you want a second opinion on roadmap, you know where to find me."

**If they say "just auditing":** "Gotcha. So you've got execution covered but could probably use strategic guidance on how to build the actual compliance program around the audit. That's where we usually help. Should we talk?"

---

#### Objection 6: "Not sure we even need [compliance standard]. Is it mandatory?"
**What it means:** They don't understand the mandate or ROI.

**Your response:**
> "Good question. It depends on your situation. Are you serving enterprise customers? Handling regulated data [financial/health/personal]? Or just being proactive? Let me ask this: what triggered this conversation internally? Who came to you saying you need [standard]?"

**Listen:** Understand if mandate is real (customer requirement, regulation) or internal opinion. If real mandate, emphasize that. If not clear, suggest they confirm before investing.

---

#### Objection 7: "We've got this figured out. Call me in 6 months."
**What it means:** They don't see current value in talking.

**Your response:**
> "Might be. Quick question though: when's your actual audit/certification attempt scheduled? And have you already started mapping controls?"

**If they say "Next month":** "Wait—if audit is next month, you might want to talk now, not in 6 months. Can you do 30 minutes this week?"

**If they say "Q3, and we're starting now":** "Perfect. In that case, early planning is exactly right. How about we reconnect in 6 weeks—not 6 months—so we can catch any gaps before you're too far in? That way you course-correct early."

## Template 3: Breach Recovery Signal

### Best For
Companies hiring after a **publicized security incident** and rebuilding their security function.

**Indicators:** "We had an incident," "We're recovering from breach," "We're rebuilding security," "Incident response team," "New CISO hired."

**Sensitivity note:** This is a delicate situation. The company is under pressure, possibly reputational damage. Your opening must be sympathetic, not opportunistic. You're there to help them succeed in recovery, not exploit the situation.

---

### Opening (30-45 seconds)

> "Hi [Name], it's [Your Name] from [Your Company]. I noticed [Company Name] went through the incident in [month], and I saw you've hired [CISO/Security Lead]. That's the right move—rebuilding takes focus and expertise. I know this isn't the easiest time, but I've worked with teams going through recovery, and I thought it could be useful to chat about how you're approaching the rebuild and where you might need external support. ### Discovery Goal

**What you want to learn:**
- What was the scope and nature of the incident? (Data breach, system compromise, ransomware?)
- How much customer/data impact? (Reputational vs. regulatory fallout?)
- Current status: Are they still in IR (incident response) mode or moving into recovery?
- Rebuild mandate: What's the new CISO/security lead's charter? (All of security, or specific areas?)
- Timeline: When do they need to show progress to customers, regulators, board?
- What's the biggest gap: Process, people, tools, architecture?

**Why it matters:** Post-breach hiring is HIGH-SIGNAL. The company has executive mandate and budget. They've realized security matters. They're motivated. But it's also delicate—they're rebuilding trust with customers and regulators. Your value prop is helping them do that rebuild efficiently.

---

### Discovery Questions (3-5 key questions)

#### Question 1: Incident Scope & Status
**Q:** "Help me understand what happened and where you are in the recovery. How long ago was the incident, and are you still in response mode, or have you moved into rebuild?"

**Why ask:** Clarifies timing, scope, and current crisis level. Recent incident = still in crisis. Incident 6+ months ago = in rebuild mode. Different conversation.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- Recent incident (0-3 months) = Still urgent, rebuilding while managing fallout
- Older incident (3-6 months) = Moving past crisis, starting strategic rebuild
- Customer/data impact = Regulatory scrutiny, need to show progress to customers

---

#### Question 2: Impact & Regulatory Fallout
**Q:** "In terms of impact, were customers affected? Are there regulatory requirements or compliance obligations you're dealing with as part of recovery?"

**Why ask:** Reveals whether this is pure security issue or compliance/regulatory too. Compliance = deadline-driven. Customers affected = reputation recovery needed.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Yes, customers affected" = Transparency obligations, trust recovery needed
- "Regulators investigating" = Compliance obligations, possible fines
- "No customer impact, but reputational" = Brand trust recovery, board scrutiny
- "Contained, no impact" = Lower urgency, but still rebuilding

---

#### Question 3: Leadership & Mandate
**Q:** "Tell me about the new security leader you've brought on. Are they building out a full security function, or focusing on specific areas like incident response or infrastructure security?"

**Why ask:** Reveals scope of rebuild and whether it's wholesale transformation or focused fix.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Full CISO hire" = Building entire security function; big mandate
- "IR-focused hire" = Incident response only; might miss broader security
- "Technical hire (architect/engineer)" = Building capabilities, but needs strategic leadership above them

---

#### Question 4: Rebuild Priorities
**Q:** "If you prioritize the rebuild—what comes first? Is it preventing the same thing from happening again, building compliance posture, or restoring customer trust?"

**Why ask:** Clarifies their strategy. This reveals what they think is most important and what might be underfunded.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Prevent same thing" = Technical focus (close specific vulnerability)
- "Compliance posture" = Regulatory/systematic focus
- "Customer trust" = Transparency/process/communication focus
- Usually it should be all three; see if they understand the complexity

---

#### Question 5: Timeline & Pressure
**Q:** "What's the timeline for showing recovery? Do you have board expectations, customer communications deadlines, or regulatory timelines?"

**Why ask:** Reveals urgency and decision-making speed. Tight timelines = motivated, will move fast.

**How to listen:** Look for:
- "Quarterly board updates" = Pressure to show progress every 90 days
- "Customer attestations needed" = Ongoing pressure to prove security
- "Regulatory deadline" = Hard deadline for compliance/remediation
- Timeline = Urgency level

### 5-Step Call Arc

#### Step 1: Acknowledge & Empathize (1-2 min)
**You:** Open with acknowledgment of the incident, not judgment.
- "I know [incident] was tough. Good on you for bringing on [new leader]."
- "Rebuilding trust is hard work. You're doing the right things."
- "This kind of situation requires focus and expertise. I wanted to reach out and see if I can help."

**Prospect:** Appreciates empathy, not exploitation.

**Tone is critical here:** You're helping them succeed, not selling solutions to their crisis.

#### Step 2: Discovery (8-10 min)
**You:** Ask questions 1-3 above.
- Listen 70%, talk 30%
- Understand incident scope, recovery stage, and new leader's mandate

**Prospect:** Explains incident, current state, rebuild priorities.

**Key listening:** What's the biggest internal debate? (Prevention vs. compliance vs. trust?) That's where the pain is.

---

#### Step 3: Probe Rebuild Reality (5-7 min)
**You:** Understand realistic rebuild timeline.
- "So you've got [new leader] starting in [when]. How much time did they have to come up to speed before they're expected to drive changes?"
- "In a rebuild like this, are you trying to do everything at once, or are you phasing it?"
- "What's the biggest challenge they're going to face—lack of team, unclear priorities, competing demands?"

**Prospect:** Gets real about rebuild complexity.

---

#### Step 4: Suggest Rebuild Approach (3-5 min)
**You:** Share how others approached rebuild.
- "Most companies in recovery phase follow this pattern: First 30 days, diagnose what went wrong and build roadmap. Next 60-90 days, implement quick wins. Then 6-12 months, systematic rebuild."
- "The companies that recover fastest tend to do: Clear root cause analysis, systematic prevention, credible transparency with customers, compliance/audit readiness."
- "The biggest mistake I see: rushing into tooling before you know what you're trying to prevent. Usually helpful to step back first, even though there's pressure to 'do something.'"

**Prospect:** Sees you've helped others recover successfully.

**Important:** Position as guide helping them think clearly, not salesperson pushing solutions.

---

#### Step 5: Close & Next Step (2 min)
**You:** "I think there's real value in understanding your rebuild strategy and seeing where you might need external support—whether that's architecture review, recovery roadmap, customer communication support, or something else. Should we grab 30 minutes with [new leader] to map that out?"

**Prospect:** Says yes or gives condition.

**If yes:** "Perfect. I'll coordinate with [new leader] directly. Before we talk, any specific area you want me focused on—incident prevention, compliance, customer trust, team building?"

**If "maybe, we're still figuring things out":** "Makes sense. You're still in discovery mode. Can I share [resource: 'Security Incident Recovery Checklist' or 'Rebuild Roadmap Template']? And we can reconnect in 2-3 weeks once you've had time to think?"

### Common Objections & Responses

#### Objection 1: "We have an external IR firm; we've got recovery covered."
**What it means:** They hired emergency response experts. They think they're good.

**Your response:**
> "That's smart—you got the right experts in for the immediate response. Here's what I've seen: IR firms are good at 'what happened and how to prevent it.' But they often don't stick around for the rebuild phase—the process changes, team building, customer trust recovery. That's where internal expertise is critical. Is your new [CISO/leader] building out a team internally, or are you still figuring that out?"

**Why it works:** Acknowledge IR firm value, but show you're talking about a different phase (rebuild vs. response).

---

**What it means:** They're security-sensitive about incident details (understandably).

**Your response:**

I'm more interested in understanding your rebuild strategy and whether you need support with [specific: architecture, team building, customer communications, compliance]. We don't need to rehash the incident; we're focused on the future."

**Why it works:** Respect privacy, shift to future.

---

#### Objection 3: "We're still in crisis mode; not ready to talk strategy yet."
**What it means:** They're slammed with incident response, not ready for external conversation.

**Your response:**
> "Fair. You're in the thick of it. Here's my suggestion: let's catch up in 4-6 weeks, once you've moved past immediate crisis and can actually think strategically. No point talking now if you're firefighting. Does end of Q3 / early Q3 work?"

**Why it works:** Respect their timing, but keep the door open.

---

#### Objection 4: "We're handling rebuild internally; don't think we need outside help."
**What it means:** They think they can do it alone.

**Your response:**
> "Could be. Depends on your internal capabilities. Quick question though: between the incident itself and now, have your people had time to think about the rebuild roadmap? Or are they still firefighting details?"

**If they say "already have a plan":** "Great. If you ever want a second set of eyes on that plan—just to validate you're hitting the key areas—happy to do a quick review."

**If they say "still figuring it out":** "That's the thing—teams are usually so deep in incident details that stepping back to think strategically is hard. Sometimes an external perspective helps you avoid the common pitfalls faster."

---

#### Objection 5: "This is a sensitive topic internally; we're not ready to engage vendors."
**What it means:** Incident is still politically/organizationally sensitive; trust is low.

**Your response:**
> "Understood. Incidents are messy, and there's usually a lot of internal dynamics happening. No pressure to engage now. But if you get to a point where external support would help—whether that's credibility, validation, or just capacity—you know where to find me. Here's my direct contact."

**Why it works:** Respect sensitivity, position as future resource, not current pressure.

---

#### Objection 6: "Board is asking for external validation; we might need someone."
**What it means:** They WILL need external help, but didn't think about it yet.

**Your response:**
> "That's actually smart governance. Board oversight on recovery is important. Two things: what kind of validation is the board looking for [architecture review, audit readiness, third-party assessment?], and when do they want it? That'll help me understand what kind of engagement would be most useful."

**Why it works:** Acknowledge board value, help them define what's needed.

---

#### Objection 7: "How do I know you can help us? What's your recovery experience?"
**What it means:** Skeptical, want proof you understand their situation.

**Your response:**
> "[Brief, specific example]: We worked with [similar company] post-[similar incident]. They had rebuilt their incident response and infrastructure security over 6 months. Key things we helped with: architecture review, team structure design, customer communication strategy. What areas are you most worried about—similar things, or different?"

**Why it works:** Give specific example, shift to understanding their needs.

## Common Patterns Across All Templates

### The 70/30 Rule
- You should talk 30% of the time
- Prospect should talk 70% of the time
- Your talk time: Opening + Discovery questions + Light suggestion

- **Not ready:** Note timeline, follow up in 4-6 weeks

### Email vs. Phone Sequencing
- **Email first** if you only have email (introduce yourself, reference company/signal, ask for call)
- **Phone better** if you have phone (warmer, more personal, harder to ignore)
- **LinkedIn if no contact** (slower, but less blocked than cold email)
